We opened mid-escape, our heroes split across two routes and running hard.

Team Tunnels hit the rumrunner's passage at full speed. Leon, Thorn, Freddie, Ty, and Lulu moved fast through the dark with Quin and Doyle's portraits in hand. A large portcullis blocked their path. Thorn grabbed the bars and wrenched the thing open by force, no finesse required. Further down, a handful of guards tried to stop them. They didn't. Our heroes went through them cleanly, took no damage, and kept moving.

Team Streets had a harder time of it. Veil, Sunder, and Little Shit slipped into the shadows and moved as quietly as they could through the marble district. Behind them, Doragnes had gone back inside and done exactly what a woman like Doragnes would do: filed the proper paperwork. An alarm went out. Some kind of protocol activated, pulling the fiends and undead of the Potter's Field into a coordinated search pattern. The streets got dangerous fast.

Little Shit stopped them at a junction. He had a problem. Competing allegiances were pulling him in two directions and he couldn't serve both. He needed a firmer arrangement, something that would override what the Pallbearer held over him. What he wanted was simple: letters of recommendation from the party for use on the material plane, and a mentor. Someone to show him how things worked up there. He looked at Veil. Veil, very hesitantly, agreed to take him on as a student of spying. Little Shit locked it in before she could change her mind, and then they were moving again. Fastest route to the docks. He had a future now.

Our heroes reconvened at the pier. The relief lasted about half a second. A critically bad luck check put them exactly where they didn't want to be: face to face with the Pallbearer himself.

He was standing on the white marble dock, the Dancing Dirge frozen behind him, Alistair motionless at the helm. A spirit naga coiled beneath the Pallbearer like a living throne. Four death knights flanked him. He didn't look angry. He looked like a man who had come to settle an account.

The offer was simple. They could leave with Quin and Doyle. Free and clear. No pursuit, no retaliation. The price was Captain Alistair. The one soul that got away. The one open account on the Pallbearer's books. He was offering to close everything in a single transaction.

Our heroes said no. Combat kicked off.

Sunder charged forward and locked down one death knight. Thorn did the same on the opposite flank. Veil squared up with a third. And Ty, seeing the board for what it was, went for the clever play. He cast Mass Suggestion, catching one death knight, the spirit naga, and the Pallbearer himself. The suggestion: help us escape. The Pallbearer burned a legendary resistance to shake it off. The death knight and the naga did not.

From the naga, now temporarily an ally, they learned she was a summoned steed bound to the Pallbearer's will. But if his legendary resistances were spent, she could land a Hold Person and lock him down. Our heroes had their plan. Burn through his defenses, then let the naga do the rest.

Veil discovered something along the way. When a death knight fell, its body exploded into platinum. Actual currency, scattering across the marble dock. Several of our heroes suddenly had a different look in their eyes.

Ty and Leon went to work on the Pallbearer's resistances. Spell after spell, forcing saves, burning through his defenses one by one. When the last resistance fell, Veil moved in, stunned him, and locked him in a grapple. Ty followed up with a Psychic Lance that left the Pallbearer badly wounded.

Leon saw his opening. The Pallbearer was crippled, held, and dying. And when he dropped, there'd be platinum. Leon moved in for the killing blow.

He never saw what hit him. The Pallbearer was a Dread Lord, the Darklord of this domain, and his death carried a contingency. Power Word Kill. No attack roll, no warning. Leon failed his save and dropped where he stood. Dead. Not unconscious, not dying. Dead.

The fight didn't stop. The remaining death knight nearly killed Ty before Freddie held the line and kept him breathing. Our heroes mopped up what they could, dragged Leon's body aboard the Dancing Dirge, and turned to the only option left.

Ty performed the resurrection ritual.

Freddie stepped in first. His divine connection to Erathis steadied the magic and lowered the threshold from ten to seven. Thorn, uncertain where he could help, reached for the natural world, for whatever thread of life still ran through this place. It wasn't much, but it was enough. Seven became six. Then Veil pulled out the portrait of Art Doyle and held it before Leon's body. She asked Doyle to speak to him.

We cut to Leon. He was standing on an estate overlooking a beautiful apple orchard and an elk farm. Peaceful. Quiet. Doyle was there, and the two of them talked. Not gently. They sparred over whether any of it was worth going back for. Whether the system was broken beyond fixing, or if there was still something to salvage. Doyle had spent eleven years in a frame asking himself the same question. Leon leaned toward returning. He wasn't sure, but he leaned that way.

The DC dropped to three.

Leon's player nominated Ty's player to make the final roll.

It was a two.

We leave our heroes on the deck of the Dancing Dirge. Ty and Alistair still have not learned the truth of their connection, and when we return, they will. They have Quin. They have Doyle. They have the compass to escape the Shadowfell, and they will use it. But Leon is gone. Not unconscious, not stable, not coming back on a better roll. Gone. The paladin who walked into the Shadowfell to find justice walked into a killing blow reaching for platinum, and the dice said no.

What comes next for Leon, if anything, will not be resurrection. It will be something else. Something not quite right. But that is a question for next session.
