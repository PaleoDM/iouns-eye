We opened with our heroes arriving at the docks of the Potter's Field, leviathan blood still drying on their skin. The disguise was working. Every soul in the district perceived them as undead, though more than a few commented on how rosy their complexion was, how close to life they still seemed.

At the customs house, they met the dock agent: a wispy-haired ghostly accountant, all business, flanked by an impish assistant with a long nose, pointy ears, and a terrible disposition. The bureaucracy of death, it turns out, is still bureaucracy. Our heroes quickly learned the local economy runs not on gold but on soul gemstones. The denominations are Plebeians, Proletariats (Prolos for short), and Poets, scaling by tens. A Poet is the largest: a single gemstone containing the soul of a creator, an artist, a builder. Our heroes inspected one and found a beautiful opera singer and dancer. Another held an architect. The commerce here is exactly what it sounds like. Souls, bought and sold like grain.

Critically, the dock agent recognized Leon. Not by name, not by face, but by station. "Didn't think you were due back so soon," he said, and mentioned that Leon's suite at the Penultimate Rest was of course still available. The tone was casual, familiar, the kind of thing you say to a regular. Nobody here can tell one Justicar from another, so they didn't know if Leon was new or returning, but either way, his presence was treated as entirely normal. Justicars come and go from the Potter's Field.

Our heroes paid roughly 300 gold each for permits to keep their magic items, then several of them converted 100 gold into a single Poet to have local currency on hand. Ty, in perhaps the shrewdest move of the session, paid five Plebeians to hire the impish creature at the docks, who goes by **Little Shit**, as their guide. It was money well spent. Little Shit is well connected and knows his way around the district.

As they got their bearings, Veil, Sunder, and Freddie put their heads together. The soul trade reminded them of something they'd already been tracking: the Cassini Brothers and their role laundering souls on behalf of the Justicars. Veil remembered that her own brother had been implicated in that work, possibly as a target. The pieces were lining up.

Three destinations presented themselves. The Intake Hall, where souls are distilled and placed into gemstones. The Exchange Floor, where the buying and selling happens in earnest. And the Actuarium, a library-like hall of ledgers where every soul that comes and goes is cataloged. Boring, meticulous, and exactly the kind of place where you find evidence. Our heroes chose the Actuarium.

Inside, they discovered another Justicar was already there. The woman at the front desk offered to take Leon in to meet his colleague, and Leon accepted. They were escorted to an out-of-the-way wing where the records from Rifthaven, and perhaps the material plane more broadly, seemed to be kept.

They eavesdropped first. The Justicar, accompanied by two imps, was searching for something she kept describing as a "metaphysical anomaly." It didn't take long before they heard the name: **Galton Quincrux**. This Justicar was hunting for Quin's soul.

Leon stepped forward and confronted her. They proved their identities to one another by drawing their swords, and the woman revealed herself to be no ordinary Justicar but **Divine Judge Josephine Maren**, one of the seven High Justicars whose names are inscribed on Leon's blade. She showed very little when she saw her own name on his steel.

What followed was a recruitment pitch. Josephine told Leon this was the happiest of days. That everything had been done correctly, by the book. That the sword was bringing him back to them to rise as the eighth Justicar and take his place in the inner circle. She was good. She was convincing.

Leon didn't roll well on his insight. But he knew in his gut that something was wrong. He had never collected on Quin's soul. He had never actually finished the job. The math didn't add up, and Leon trusted that over whatever she was selling.

"Remove your helmet," she said. "Let me gaze upon you so we may finalize the ritual."

Leon caught it. The invisible blade she'd readied, aimed at the gap in his armor where the coif wouldn't cover his collarbone. Instead of complying, he threw out his wings and drew steel.

The imps each consumed a pair of Poets and swelled into spined devils. Ty dropped a Sickening Radiance on both of them immediately. Freddie threw up Spirit Guardians. And then Thorn and Veil proceeded to play the most brutal game of pinball the Potter's Field has ever seen, shoving and dragging the devils back and forth through both zones of effect, forcing save after save as the overlapping magic chewed them apart. It was devastatingly effective.

Leon took the Divine Judge one-on-one. She hit hard, but Leon's aasimar ancestry grants him resistance to both necrotic and radiant damage, and Josephine's arsenal leaned heavily on both. He weathered her worst and answered with smite after smite. When Thorn finished with the devils and turned his attention to the Judge, the tide turned for good. Leon drove the killing blow home.

As she fell, Josephine mentioned something called resurrection insurance, and made clear she didn't consider this over. Veil's response was immediate: burn the bodies.

In the aftermath, our heroes searched what they could. The ledger Josephine had been holding when combat broke out confirmed what they needed to know: Quin's soul, the metaphysical anomaly she'd been hunting, is here in the Potter's Field. Specifically, it's held in the Pallbearer's personal collection, a place called the **Galleria**.

We leave our heroes in the quiet wing of the Atrium, two missions now overlapping into one. Find the smoking gun that ties the Justicars to the Pallbearer. And heist Quin's soul out of the Galleria so that a good man can finally, properly rest.
