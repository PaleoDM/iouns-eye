We opened on the deck of the Dancing Dirge, our heroes crowded around the diamond Ty had used for the resurrection ritual. Leon was in there. Not as a soul, not as a ghost. As a gemstone. A Pleb, specifically. Not even a Prolo. Certainly not a Poet. The luck check came back a seven, and that was the best they were going to get. But he could talk. He could see and hear. And for a group that had watched him drop dead on a marble dock reaching for platinum, that was enough. The mood was relief cut with comedy. Leon, the Divine Judge, a budget gemstone with opinions.

Ty and Captain Alistair finally had the conversation. It started with questions. Ty wanted to know about Cliff and Roe, about the life they'd built in Rifthaven, about the operation they'd run. Alistair was happy to talk. He called Cliff "Cliffy", or sometimes even "Cliff-Bear." Ty was't exactly comfortable with it all, but he kept listening. And gained a new respect for the man. Cliff wasn't just an accountant. He was the only man in Rifthaven brave enough to take on the city's accountants and win, cooking the books clean enough to fund and conceal an underground railroad that smuggled slaves out of the Underdark. Roe ran the apothecary that kept Alistair's ship legally employed. Together, they built a network that saved lives. Ty left the conversation with a new understanding of his father, even if "Cliff-Bear" was going to take some getting used to.

The Dancing Dirge carried them home. Rifthaven was not as they'd left it.

Haji had been pressing the bastion hard. Whatever our heroes had brought back from the Shadowfell, he wanted it found, and he was squeezing Sparkworks to get it. Captain Reeve had been holding the line, keeping people safe and the operation tight, but the pressure was building. Arden was busy. He'd thrown himself into a political campaign, running for office, with Jerusha managing the fundraising effort and Lord Harwin preparing to serve as running mate. The bastion had kept moving without them, but the cracks were showing.

Phoebe found Ty, and it was emotion. She wanted him home. She told him so, plainly, emotionally, the way someone does when they've been watching the person they care about walk further and further into danger. Ty...responding with jokes nad hijinks. And explained that there was still work to do. It was not the answer she wanted. 

Then Freddie and Ty got to work on Leon. The diamond was a starting point, not a destination. What came back was not exactly the paladin who had walked into the Shadowfell. It was closer to a construct than a creature, and whatever it was, it was piloting Leon's armor. One wing broken, the other functional. A slightly undead disposition. More machine than man. But he was back, and he was Leon, and that would have to be enough.

The bastion didn't stay quiet for long. Fireballs hit in the distance and then the walls were breached. Yugoloths poured in. Thorn was captured almost immediately, pulled into an arcane tome before he could plant his feet. The rest of the party took a powerful stunning blast, but Leon's paladin aura held. Saves were made across the board.

What followed was efficient. Ty opened with Chromatic Orb and put serious damage on the field. Veil locked down most of the enemies and then turned her attention to the arcane tome, destroying it and freeing Thorn. A nasty Wall of Fire made positioning difficult, but our heroes worked through it with the kind of coordination that comes from months of practice. The yugoloths went down. The bastion was safe. Home was home again, at least for now.

With the dust settled, our heroes sat down with the portrait of Art Doyle. Doyle was not friendly. He was not exactly grateful. He looked at Leon the way a professor looks at a student who hasn't earned the grade yet. Prove it, was the message. Prove you're truly good. But he talked.

The Aletheia procedure, Doyle explained, has a failsafe built into it. Two institutions have the authority to impose checks and balances: the Order of the Ju Shi and the High Haven Arcane Academy. If high-ranking members of both institutions invoke a vote of no confidence, it triggers a governmental review. The procedure freezes until the review is complete. No more sentences, no more executions, no more quiet disappearances into soul gemstones.

The catch was the word "high-ranking." They'd need serious people willing to put their names on the line.

Our heroes already had their targets. Gideon, Veil's brother, holds a department chair in the school of necromancy at High Haven. He's their play at the Academy. Sir Sunder would handle the Order of the Ju Shi, assuming they can get past Divine Judge and monk Vine Ashen Bloom, who stands between them and the door.

Two institutions. Two conversations. One shot at shutting the whole thing down.

We leave our heroes having made their choice. Gideon first. The brother Veil found an empty portrait frame for in the Pallbearer's gallery. The brother whose spot was reserved, waiting. Whatever that conversation looks like, it starts next session.
