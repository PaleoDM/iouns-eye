We opened in the alley behind Giuseppe's Toy Shop. One talisman in hand, one significant problem on the board: Maligno had the Birthday Host, and the second talisman. Before they moved, they took a moment to collect themselves — assess what they had, what they needed, and what was still weighing on them.

Sir Sunder used the quiet to pull Veil aside. He had been a little odd since arriving in this place, and now he spoke his worries plainly: had he failed Veil? Had the years spent training her in the temple, never venturing out, never truly going on missions, had that done her a disservice? Veil stared as though she could hardly engage with the question. She didn't understand what he was getting at. None of this had ever been a burden she was carrying. It hadn't even occurred to her.

Which, finally, led poor Sunder to understand. He had been projecting—offering Veil a guilt she had no interest in accepting, worrying about a wound she didn't have. The real thing he'd been circling around was simpler and more personal: he had enjoyed this. The mission. The danger. The alleyways in the Shadowfell and the chaos of a real fight. It made him feel young again. He had liked it, and he hadn't quite given himself permission to admit that. But here, in a dying domain ruled by a grieving wooden boy, he finally did.

The moment he let go of it, the glamour fell away. The adolescent form shed like a second skin, and Sir Sunder stood again as he truly was — older, weathered, unmistakably himself. It was a small thing, and a large one.

With Sunder settled, the group turned to Ty, who had a problem of his own. Giuseppe's warning still hung over him: the mists had recognized something unresolved, and he would not be able to leave Odaire until that changed. Ty already knew what it was. He had been carrying guilt over Quin's death since the beholder fight — a quiet, persistent weight that had never quite left him. So Lulu agreed to try a communion, leaning into the thinness of the veil here in the Shadowfell. It worked better than she expected. Quin came through clearly.

He was fine. Not great, not miserable — just vaguely dissatisfied, in the way that a man who could always calculate the answer to any problem might be dissatisfied by a place where there are no more problems left to calculate. There was a kind of cosmic irony to it that Quin would have appreciated, if he weren't the one experiencing it. But crucially: he had seen every thread, every possible outcome, and he drew no connection between Ty and his own death. None. He said as much.

"I wish there was a coin I could flip," Quin mused. "Or a roll of a die I could push from over here."

He had no idea that a certain Faerie Godmother was nearby, and that a wish in her proximity is never entirely unheard. From beyond the grave, Quin was granted one final portent. Classic Quin: he rolled a natural twenty.

Something loosened in Ty's chest. With both Sunder and Ty having shed what the mists had been holding against them, the way out was theoretically clear. Our heroes chose not to take it quietly, for Ty felt he still had unfinished business in needing to rescue the Birthday Host. She had given herself up to spare the other children. That was not something this group was going to simply walk away from. They made their way to the theater.

What awaited them there was, in a word, daunting. Archers with high ground, armored bruisers on the floor, body swapping puppets at the flanks, and a pair of massive stone golems standing like pillars. The kind of fight where the math doesn't particularly favor you.

Sunder and Thorn did not care about the math.

Between the two of them, they absorbed the better part of four hundred points of damage — an almost incomprehensible display of durability. Sunder carved through the front line with crippling precision. Thorn did so with a ferocity unrivaled. Leon re-upped Crusader's Mantle and burned it across the whole party, giving everyone that extra bite of radiant on every hit, then personally delivered a devastating critical strike to one of the stone golems. While Thunder and Sunder locked down both golems and turned the front line into a wall. Freddie held the whole enterprise together from behind — a crucial Mass Healing Word kept the front from collapsing, and several other well-timed interventions meant that all that punishment absorbed didn't translate into bodies on the ground.

At the back, Ty summoned a celestial spirit and settled into artillery. When the moment came, he reached for one of Quin's chromatic orbs and called in the favor the universe owed him. The portent landed as a natural twenty. The orb detonated against the crossbeams supporting Maligno and exploded outward in a vicious ricochet, arcing across the theater and catching enemies in a cascade of color and force. Maligno's puppet wires severed, the terrible boy went limp, paralyzing the monster temporarily.

Veil was already moving.

She crossed the battlefield at full sprint and pulled the calling card from Maligno's still form — the talisman attuned to the Pallbearer's domain, pried off him in one clean motion. As Maligno finally came to ad prepared to launch an attack, he rolled a critical fail. Veil's bracers fired off, crippling the poppet once more and robbing him of his attacks. This led Veil, and the Birthday Host, to laugh at the sad, sorry little construct who wasn't even a real boy. In a fit of rage, Maligno launched forward towards the others. Veil and the Birthday Host didn't stick around to finish the job. They didn't need to. The moment the group reconvened around Veil, they called upon the mists and left Odaire behind — fully, truly, without looking back.

The last sound that followed them out was the soft creak of Giuseppe's footsteps making their way into the theater. An old man, going to repair the thing he made, because he simply cannot stop.

In the grey quiet of the mists, our heroes finally had room to breathe. Two talismans in hand now: the compass that could return them to Shadow Rifthaven, and the calling card that would take them forward to the Pallbearer's domain—the Potter's Field.

Not everyone was ready to move immediately.

Leon found himself approached by one of the Odaire urchins. The child pressed him: if Maligno's word had been law in his domain, why didn't it count? Why did they have the right to defy it? Leon, still the Justicar underneath everything, argued that not all laws are just — that law and justice are not always the same thing. The urchin listened. Then the disguise slipped, and the Faerie Godmother looked back at Leon with ancient eyes. She drew her own blood with the edge of his blade and inscribed seven names into the steel — the seven high Justicars, the heads of the Aletheia protocol. Then she told him: *when you have decided what you truly believe in, and you know how to tell when the law is real, the blade will be ready.*

Leon was all in. He had a new charge now, seven names sharp in the steel and a question that needed answering.

Meanwhile, Veil, Freddie, and Ty sat with the Birthday Host. She was free of Odaire, but she had no name none of the children there did—and no idea where to go. Our heroes pointed her toward Rifthaven: Sparkworks, Captain Reeve, the bastions. She'd find work, and she'd find people who'd look out for her. As for a name: Sir Sunder gave it to her. Having watched Veil quietly claim the girl as a potential addition to her spy network, he looked at this young woman—closer to nineteen with her glamour lifted, all that Odaire time finally unwinding from her—and dubbed her Turmoil. May she resolve her own, and bring it to her enemies.

Before committing to the forward push, Freddie sat in commune with Erathis. Three questions, three answers. Is it safe to rest in the mists? Yes. Will it be safe to rest in the Pallbearer's domain? Never. Does the Pallbearer know you're coming? No.

That settled it. We leave our heroes choosing to rest while they can, full hit points and spell slots restored, two talismans in hand, and the Potter's Field waiting somewhere ahead in the dark.
