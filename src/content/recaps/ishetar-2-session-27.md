We opened with our heroes finalizing their downtime in Ishetar, and the quieter but important work of tending to things that matter.

Lyra spent her downtime casting *Sending* spells across Wanun — first to the clergy in Tusketaan, and then all the way west to her home temple in Laenora, beyond the Aroiin Empire. She had questions about Kato, the Dawnfather priest whose heavy-handed ministry had upended Vicas's life, and the answers painted a complicated picture. A fringe sect of the Dawnfather's faithful had splintered off in Tusketaan, courting marginalized gnomish communities who traditionally worship Glittergold. These priests had taken a peculiarly literal view of the Dawnfather's golden light — emphasizing not just the sun, but maize, wheat, and gold itself — and in doing so had begun siphoning followers from Glittergold's flock. The church of Glittergold had already lodged a formal complaint. Lyra, armed with what she'd learned from Vicas, filed one of her own. Nothing immediately actionable, her mentor assured her, but a speaking mirror would be sent to Ishetar for more careful communication across the continent.

From there, we checked in with each of our heroes in turn.

Azrael opened up about the shadow that has followed him his entire life: the cult that raised him, now formally known as the **Order of the Celestial Seed**. Its members — Elders, Brides, Keepers, Seedlings, and Vessels — haunt his thoughts. Azrael himself is an escaped Vessel, and the Order remains a constant, gnawing source of fear. He doesn't talk about it often, but it was clear this is a weight he carries every day.

Vicas, when pressed on his training and Azrael's earlier plea to "stick around until the springs wear down," admitted that he hasn't been thinking much about the future. The present demands too much. The growing magical threats bearing down on Ishetar weigh heavily on him, and his focus has been singular: either he protects this town from whatever comes, or he makes damn sure the people he leaves behind can. And underneath it all, quietly, selfishly — the thought still lingers that a hero's death might be what Vicas truly wants for himself.

Thump continues to nurture young Mikkel's budding elemental magic — not through explicit instruction, but through the quiet example of a druid who believes that these gifts belong to the community. Magic, in Thump's view, is something you share freely.

And Rooker reckoned with the fact that Sombra had stolen authorship credit on their joint publication — the groundbreaking thesis on ritualistic soul repair. He wrestled with it for a moment, genuinely feeling the sting, but then landed somewhere only Rooker could: game recognizes game. He'd have done the same thing.

Then we slowed down for a pair of beautiful character scenes.

Thump sat with Vicas and tried, gently, to reach him. "You're not one to mourn in a religious sense," he said, and asked what color most reminded Vicas of Shallah. He offered to prepare a quiet place for meditation — not a temple, nothing formal, just a space. Vicas, vulnerable in a way we rarely see, confided that he doesn't actually remember whether Shallah had asked him to do what he did. Whether she'd spoken the words. Thump answered flatly: "I don't believe that." He didn't push. He didn't need to. He simply insisted that Vicas had known her — truly known her — and had known what she needed. The words didn't need to be spoken aloud.

Elsewhere, Azrael approached Lyra with a humble request: teach him how to brew a proper cup of tea. He'd tried once, to comfort Vicas, and it had gone terribly. What followed was a warm, unhurried conversation about community, faith, and what the tea ceremonies mean to Lyra — how she came to the Dawnfather, what service looks like, and why the small rituals matter as much as the grand ones. By the end, Azrael was seated in Lyra's garden, learning the names and natures of every herb and leaf that goes into the teas she prepares. A quiet scene that said everything about how far these two have come.

Then things took a turn.

Rooker raised the question of Bucephalus. With his magic fundamentally changed, Rooker can no longer summon the old legionnaire — the match simply doesn't work anymore. Lyra cast *Speak with Dead* on the skull and uncovered a thorny problem: Bucephalus had sworn a binding oath in a place where oaths are magically permanent, pledging to protect the people of Ekkorai *forever*. Until that oath was fulfilled or redirected, neither rest nor release was possible. Lyra gently suggested that perhaps his job was already done. Bucephalus disagreed — and tested the theory by casting a powerful spell on Rooker. Rooker failed the save. "Definitely not ready," Bucephalus concluded. His job was far from finished.

Uncertain what to do, Lyra set about a powerful ritual — *Remove Curse* layered with *Animate Dead* — and the banshee from before drifted through the veil once more, drawn by the magic and the company. She and Lyra spoke about the nature of the stubborn dead, those too bound by duty to let go. And through the ritual, combined with a beautiful dirge from the banshee, something extraordinary happened. Both Bucephalus and the skull from the Red Vale were transformed into **Dullahan** — headless horsemen who wield their own skulls as shields and crack their spinal cords as brimstone whips, each astride a steed of hellfire and black death. Shepherds of souls. Harbingers of death. Created by the banshee herself, they were set loose along Ishetar's perimeter to patrol the surrounding wilds and guard against the undead. Both legionnaires, satisfied at last to have a means of fulfilling their oath, took to their new duty with grim purpose.

With the spiritual matters settled, Vero stepped in to test whether our heroes — and Rooker in particular — were ready to go back out into the world. They'd been tinkering on a set of construct contraptions designed to defend Ishetar, and figured they could kill two birds with one stone. Four constructs were unleashed in the training pit: punchers, archers, and at least one rigged with explosives. Rooker did his best to avoid using magic entirely, though he broke glass on a clutch *Mage Armor* that absorbed a blow that would have laid him out. Thump struggled to land damage for most of the fight but clinched the final kill with a well-placed *Grasping Vine*. Lyra tested her new morningstar — the radiant sun forged by Vero and Deft — and it sang in her hands, keying beautifully off her wisdom and dealing extra damage. Vicas, unable to poison the constructs, found a workaround through Verity's corruption that chipped away at them all the same.

Then came the war council.

Lady Rowena and Hrothgar laid out the situation with the Turquoise Timber Trust in stark terms. Conflict was inevitable — the only question was how. Rowena was adamant: civilians could not be harmed, and could not be made to feel Ishetar was the aggressor. Hrothgar, when Rooker asked whether Ishetar could hold without them, answered with characteristic bluntness — yes, but it would mean kin killing kin. The worst kind of fight. By his count, the Trust housed two hundred to two hundred and forty souls, but only thirty to forty were actual combatants, many of them hired sellswords with no particular loyalty to the cause. If things could be turned, those numbers might shift.

As they strategized, Thump noticed and intercepted a suspicious-looking bird. It proved to be an animal messenger — a spy, sent to deliver a message and eavesdrop in turn. Thump dispelled its magical compulsion and spoke with it plainly. The bird carried a message from Timbermaster **Brenna Tolvane** to Lady Rowena: put our differences aside, meet at the **Restful Lily** in four days' time. "Bring your champions, and I'll bring mine."

Thump also coaxed out what the bird had seen near the Myreshade. Things were bad down south. The bird spoke in terrified tones of massive metal-skeleton monsters — terrible, dangerous creatures that stalked the forest. The Trust's druids, the bird explained, harvest sap and blood from the local wildlife to protect them from these beasts. Our heroes, of course, understood immediately what was really going on. The "monsters" were logging equipment. The blood harvesting was the real predation — the Trust's druids draining the life force of the local ecosystem under the pretense of protection.

Our heroes settled in to plan. They liked one idea in particular: don't wait for the meeting. Don't invite the enemy anywhere near Ishetar. Teleport to the Restful Lily immediately, then march hard and arrive at the Turquoise Timber Trust in a single day — three days before anyone expects them. Take the conversation directly to Brenna Tolvane's doorstep and put them on the back foot before they've had time to prepare. Rowena would stay behind with the garrison to defend Ishetar.

We leave our heroes on the night of the 12th of Bahamut, finalizing their plans and preparing to march south. The Turquoise Timber Trust expects a diplomatic meeting in four days. Our heroes intend to be there in one.
